AVIONICS

  • Electromechanical Integration Team - Rohil Agarwal 23', Annie Chu 22'

    Recovery Module Test PCB:

    Once the rocket reaches apogee (10,000 feet), this PCB will send commands from the radio to the nichrome wire and solenoid valves, which will deploy the main and drogue parachutes, respectively. This board design will be used for ground tests. For the final versions of the board that will actually go on the rocket, the radio module, programming module, and microcontroller will be placed on other boards, and this PCB will basically serve as a switch.

    Progress:

    The first design of the recovery board has been manufactured and is in the process of being populated.

  • Power Team - Dhara Patel 22'

    Power Test PCB:

    Our rocket will be powered by a 12V lithium-ion battery pack. The battery pack is split into 2 independent groups that are in parallel. Each group contains 3 cells that are in series. There are many factors that determine a good battery management system. We are choosing to focus on providing telemetry, safety faults (temperature, over-voltage, and over-current protection), and passive cell balancing. We will be using the MAX1647 to charge and discharge the battery pack.

    Progress:

    The first iteration of the battery management system is under final review. After review it will be manufactured and tested.

  • Communications Team - Regan Mah 23'

    Communications Test PCB:

    Communications PCB is using the LoRa radio to transfer information from the rocket to the main computer. As the rocket is in flight, the two LoRa radios will communicate with one another--one radio will take the information from the rocket’s other systems while the other will receive the data and display it for us to see.

    Progress:

    As of now, the radios are able to communicate and transfer information. Most of this semester was spent editing code and implementing it into the radios. As of now, we have not gotten to test how far of a distance the communication lasts, and most manuals don’t seem to give a definitive answer.

  • Software Team - Alex Bahner 21', Alex Butler 23'

    Main Computer:

    From its initial launch position on the pad to its final destination after launch, all systems of the avionics subteam must be able to interface with the main computer. The main computer has two primary jobs during the flight of the rocket. Foremost, the computer will interface with the sensing module to take in the information necessary to determine when to interface with the recovery module. Throughout the flight, it will also pass on necessary flight information to the ground station every second (including GPS coordinates, readouts from the sensing board, and all battery and arming information), while also writing to an onboard “blackbox” to validate the flight data.

    Progress:

    Data logger has been finished. Exploratory work into Kalman filters for onboard flight analysis for triggering the recovery payload has begun with breakout boards; however, needs lots of tuning. Firmware has been compiled for all sensing and interfacing boards with the main computer and is ready for the microcontroller and breakout boards.
